It’s hard to give a direct answer to this question. It might be easier to start with something everyone agrees on.

“Madness is rare in individuals—but in groups, parties, nations, and ages it is the rule,” Nietzsche said (before he went crazy). If you can spot a popular belief that’s wrong, you can find the hidden truth behind it.

Think about a simple idea: companies exist to make money, not to lose it. That should be clear to anyone. But in the late 1990s, many didn’t see it that way. People thought big losses were just investments in a brighter future. The popular idea of the “New Economy” said page views online were more important than making a profit.

People only realize old beliefs were wrong after they fail; when this happens, we call it a bubble. But the problems caused by bubbles don’t just go away when they burst. The internet boom of the ’90s was the biggest bubble since 1929, and what we learned from it still shapes how we think about technology today. The first step to clear thinking is to question what we believe about the past.
