Rule #2  
Embrace Boredom  

If you want to see how people get really good at focusing, check out the Knesses Yisroel Synagogue in Spring Valley, New York, at six a.m. on a weekday. You’ll probably see at least twenty cars in the parking lot. Inside, there will be a bunch of people reading and studying texts. Some might be reading quietly, whispering in an old language, while others are debating with a partner. At one side of the room, a rabbi will be leading a bigger group discussion. This early morning meeting is just a small part of the hundreds of thousands of orthodox Jews who wake up early every weekday to study important texts from their religion.  

I learned about this from Adam Marlin, a member of the Knesses Yisroel congregation who goes to the morning study group regularly. Marlin told me that his goal is to understand one page of the Talmud each day, even though he sometimes doesn’t make it. He often studies with a partner to really challenge himself.  

What I find interesting about Marlin isn’t just what he knows, but the effort he puts in to learn. When I talked to him, he mentioned how intense his morning routine is. “It’s a serious discipline, mostly about the ‘deep work’ stuff you write about,” he said. “I run a growing business, but this is often the hardest mental workout I do.” This challenge isn’t just for Marlin; it’s part of the practice. His rabbi once told him, “You can’t think you’ve done your daily duty unless you’ve really pushed your brain to its limits.”
