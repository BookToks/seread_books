Running a blog called Study Hacks has gone from a few readers to hundreds of thousands each month. But getting people’s attention online is really tough. 

However, it seems easier now. Social media has changed the game by making it less about hard work and more about quick interactions. Instead of earning attention through valuable content, it’s like a trade: “I’ll pay attention to you if you pay attention to me,” no matter if what we say is actually good or not. 

For example, a blog or magazine with the same stuff you see on Facebook or Twitter wouldn’t get any readers. But when that same stuff is shared on social media, it gets likes and comments. The deal here is that you get attention from your friends, and in return, you give them attention too, even if it’s not really deserved. You “like” my post, and I’ll “like” yours. This makes everyone feel important without putting in much effort.

If you stop using these services suddenly, you can see how much people really notice you as a content creator. For most people, the truth might be surprising—only your close friends and family will probably realize you’re gone. I know I sound a bit negative about this, but it’s important to talk about because wanting to feel important can make people waste their time and focus.

For some, taking a break from social media for thirty days will be hard and cause problems, especially if you’re a college student or an online influencer. But for most people, this break might help you see social media in a new light. These platforms aren’t as crucial as they seem; they’re just products made by companies that want to sell your information to advertisers. They can be fun, but in the big picture of your life, they’re just a small distraction that can keep you from focusing on what really matters. You won’t know how important they are to you until you take a step back.
